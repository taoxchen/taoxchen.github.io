Reimagining Study Away: A Mobile-First Experience for Williams Seniors

What if preparing to go abroad after graduation was as easy as scrolling on your phone? In our Human-Computer Interaction course, we set out to redesign the Williams College Global Education & Study Away website — with a twist. Rather than targeting sophomores or juniors studying abroad, our team focused on graduating seniors seeking fellowships, careers, or experiences overseas. With insights from user interviews, moodboards, paper sketches, and UI prototyping in Figma, we transformed a clunky, outdated site into a streamlined, visually modern, mobile-first platform. Our redesign prioritizes usability, visual clarity, and senior-specific needs, integrating Williams' branding with intuitive navigation and key postgraduate resources. In this post, we walk through our process — from ideation and user testing to the design decisions behind our final interactive prototype — and reflect on what’s next for improving senior study away experiences.

Gesturing to Play FireBoy, WaterGirl

What if you could play your favorite games without ever touching a keyboard? In our Human-Computer Interaction course, we explored the future of hands-free computing by reimagining how we interact with browser-based games. Our team — Gabe Ramirez, Luke Young-Xu, and Tao Chen — redesigned the control system of Fireboy and Watergirl, replacing traditional keyboard inputs with real-time hand gestures using the MediaPipe Gesture Recognizer framework. From brainstorming intuitive gestures to testing via the Wizard of Oz method, and ultimately building a fully functional prototype, we navigated challenges like momentum preservation, gesture pairing, and usability design. This blog post walks through our full design sprint — from application selection and gesture ideation to final implementation and reflection — showing how gesture-based interfaces can reshape the way we play, interact, and build accessible digital experiences.

Designing Reddit for Users with Cognitive and Learning Disabilities

Reddit’s chaotic homepage, dense comment threads, and jargon-heavy buttons might feel intuitive to some — but for users with ADHD, dyslexia, or autism, the platform can become an overwhelming maze. In this article, I examine Reddit’s interface through the lens of design justice, WCAG standards, and first-person accounts from neurodiverse users. What happens when we stop assuming everyone processes information the same way? By pairing accessibility guidelines with lived experience, I explore how small design shifts — from collapsible threads to icon labels — could turn Reddit from cognitively exhausting to cognitively inclusive. This post is part interface critique, part call to action: let’s build platforms that care, not just comply.

by Tao Chen on May 12, 2025

Seeing More Clearly: An AI-Powered Extension for Describing Online Images

The web is visual — but what happens when you can’t see the visuals? For millions of users with visual or cognitive impairments, the absence of alt text on images makes vast swaths of the Internet inaccessible. Alongside Amirjon Ulmasov, Adhip Rijal, and Kunal Pal, I co-designed a browser extension that bridges this gap: a lightweight tool that lets users click any image on a webpage and receive an instant AI-generated description, no uploads or logins required. In this piece, I walk through our full design process — from identifying real-world user needs and sketching interface mockups to implementing and testing our extension using Groq AI. Along the way, we encountered unexpected challenges, from data transfer issues to user interface confusion, and turned feedback from real users into tangible, accessible design improvements. Our final product isn’t just a captioning tool — it’s a commitment to inclusive, human-centered design.
